BACKGROUND AND ARTIFACT

WWTP facilities operate continuously and contain specialized structural and architectural components that must withstand aggressive environmental conditions, such as high humidity, chemical exposure, and vibration from mechanical processes. As a result, asset management are essential to ensure long term operational reliability, safety, and cost efficiency. By leveraging BIM as an information model rather than solely a geometric representation, relevant asset data can be structured, stored, and transferred to the operational phase. This enables facility owners and operators to access accurate, up to date information about building components throughout the asset lifecycle.

In relation to ensuring maintainability, there are several stakeholders, datasets, and information systems involved, which are summarized in the Appendix IV - 1 List of Stakeholder for Asset Management.

Asset Information and BIM as an Information System

For the Sludge Treatment Building, architectural and structural elements can be enriched with nongeometric information that supports maintenance and asset management.

Relevant asset parameters include:

  • Asset identification codes
  • Element type and material
  • Installation or construction completion date
  • Design life expectancy
  • Inspection and maintenance notes
  • Associated drawings and manuals

By embedding this information directly into BIM elements, the model becomes a single source of truth for asset related data. Instead of relying on fragmented drawings or spreadsheets, asset managers and facility teams can access spatially linked information through the BIM environment or the Common Data Environment. REFLECTION

  • Challenges in Developing the Artifacts

One of the main challenges was making the Revit models function properly within Autodesk Tandem. The architectural and structural models were originally developed for design and construction purposes and therefore lacked the structured asset classifications and standardized parameters required by Tandem. As a result, issues such as inconsistent naming, missing metadata, and incomplete asset hierarchies occurred during the import process, requiring manual model cleaning and parameter adjustments.

In addition, not all asset related information was transferred seamlessly from Revit to Tandem. Some maintenance relevant data had to be redefined within the asset management environment, highlighting ongoing interoperability limitations between BIM authoring tools and asset management platforms.

  • Support for Asset Management Decision Making

The maintenance and asset dashboards developed in Autodesk Tandem supported asset management decision making by providing a structured and visual overview of key asset information for the Sludge Treatment Building. This overview made it possible for the Asset Manager to identify missing, inconsistent, or incomplete data that affected maintenance planning and lifecycle assessments. By combining installation dates with maintenance intervals, the remaining service life of assets was estimated more accurately. This supported replacement planning and improved preventive maintenance scheduling, reducing dependence on reactive maintenance.

The inclusion of maintenance manuals as asset parameters supported maintenance execution by giving maintenance engineers direct access to technical documentation through the model. This reduced search time and minimized the risk of incorrect maintenance procedures. Assets were grouped by location, type, and exposure conditions, which enabled prioritization of inspections in areas subject to high humidity, chemical exposure, and vibration. Overall, the artifacts improved transparency, strengthened control over asset information, and enhanced decision-making related to maintenance and asset performance.

  • Future Improvements and Integration Opportunities

If more time and resources were available, several opportunities for further improvement and integration could enhance the asset management artifacts beyond using Autodesk Tandem as a static information repository. First, the BIM-based asset model could be integrated with a Computerized Maintenance Management System or Facility Management platform so that inspections, maintenance activities, and work orders are directly linked to the assets in the model. The next step is moving from static repository to connected Asset Information Model where conditions state update the asset record over time, through agreed information exchange (Sacks, et al., 2025). This would enable process automation, reduce manual handling of maintenance planning and execution, and minimize the risk of information loss between planning and operational phases.

In addition, the asset information system could be enhanced by developing role-based dashboards for different stakeholder needs (asset managers, facility managers, maintenance engineers), allowing customized views and more effective decision support. Furthermore, integrating cost-related data such as energy consumption, repair costs, and replacement values would enable evaluation of total cost of ownership and support financial decision-making regarding maintenance, repair, or replacement strategies. Finally, the long-term ambition would be to develop a fully integrated digital representation of the facility by combining BIM with operational and maintenance data streams, enabling continuous feedback between physical assets and the digital model for performance optimization and strategic asset management.
